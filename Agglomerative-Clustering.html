Different agglomerative (hierarchical) clustering algorithms were proposed to compute phylogenetic trees from evolutionary distances:
  • Unweighted Pair Group Method with Arithmetic Mean (average linkage) by
    Robert R. Sokal and Charles D. Michener (1958)
  • Weighted Pair Group Method with Arithmetic Mean (simple average) by Louis L. McQuitty (1967)
  • Neighbour-Joining of Naruya Saitou and Masatoshi Nei (1987) modified by James A. Studier and Karl J. Keppler (1988)
And there exists many approaches and all of them have advantages and disadvantages. For example Unweighted PGMA (or Weighted PGMA) is only then a good tree computation method, if the distances from the root to the leafs are always the same (ultrametric distance matrix). In other cases the approach is unreliable. Where from Neighbour-Joining is said that it is generally the better procedure (giving biologically more relevant results).

The principle of all agglomerative (bottom-up) approaches is always the same, in the beginning you have given a matrix of pairwise evolutionary distances between singleton clusters (containing only one element) and in this matrix $D$ or in a matrix $D^*$ derived from that you search for the entry with the lowest value $D_{min} = D_{i,j}$ (where $i \neq j$). You merge the clusters $i$ and $j$ to a new cluster $ij = i \cup j$ and recompute the distances to the remaining clusters with some distance formula $\delta_1$ given with the respective approach. Afterwards the distance between $i,j$ and the merged cluster $ij$ in the tree is recomputed with a second formula $\delta_2$. These stated steps are iterated for $O(N)$ rounds, where $N$ is number of input singleton clusters.

For the given input, the
  • changing distance matrices
  • phylogenetic tree
are computed and displayed in reversed order.
